Consumer Warning - Landscaping

"He destroyed our garden and disappeared with £5,200": Stoke Heath couple warn over rogue landscaper

Retired couple Sarah and Mark Mitchell paid a landscaper nearly a third more than his original quote after a series of unexpected extras. When they refused a further payment demand, he abandoned the job overnight and has not been seen since.
Consumer By Daniel Marsh, Consumer Affairs 5 min read
Photo: The Bromsgrove Herald
The Mitchell garden in Stoke Heath, left in a worse state than before work began. Plants were removed and not replaced, and drainage work was left half finished.

Sarah and Mark Mitchell had been planning a garden redesign at their Stoke Heath home for two years. When they found a landscaper online with an attractive portfolio and strong reviews, they felt the time was right.

The original quote was £3,800 for new planting, a relined pond, and improved drainage. Sarah, 58, said she felt reassured by the detail in the proposal. "It was very professional. He broke everything down by task and gave us a clear timeline."

Work began well. But within a week, additional charges began to appear. The soil required treatment. The drainage was more complex than anticipated. A specific plant variety was more expensive. Each request was for a few hundred pounds.

"Each one seemed reasonable on its own," said Mark, 54. "It is only when you add them up you realise what has happened."

By the time the total reached £5,200, the Mitchells refused a further demand for £800 to complete what they had originally been promised. The landscaper told them he would return after the weekend. He did not. Several established plants had been removed and not replaced. The pond liner had been taken out but not refitted. Drainage channels had been dug but left open.

Sarah described the garden as being in a worse state than before he had arrived. "We paid a man more than five thousand pounds to vandalise our garden and drive away."

"Each extra charge seemed reasonable on its own. It is only when you add them all up that you realise what has been done to you."

Sarah Mitchell, Stoke Heath

Escalating quote fraud is a well-documented tactic in the landscaping and garden trade, where it can be difficult for homeowners to judge whether additional costs are genuine or fabricated. Unlike building work, there is no formal licensing or accreditation requirement for landscapers in England, making it easier for fraudsters to operate.

"Anyone can call themselves a landscaper," said a Worcestershire Trading Standards spokesperson. "There is no legal barrier. That makes it particularly important for consumers to verify credentials independently before any work begins, rather than relying on a portfolio and a set of reviews that may not be genuine."

The Mitchells have reported the matter to Trading Standards and are taking advice on whether to pursue a civil claim. However, with no verifiable business address and payments made partly by cash and partly by bank transfer to a personal account, the prospects of recovery are uncertain.

What to do before hiring a landscaper
  • 1
    Do your due diligence. Check with your local Trading Standards for any complaints or warnings. Ask the tradesperson if they have a VIA number and verify it at viacheck.co.uk. And always ask for references from previous customers before committing to anything.
  • 2
    Never pay more than 10-20% upfront. Stagger payments to agreed milestones. Avoid cash wherever possible. Bank transfer creates a paper trail; cash leaves you with nothing if things go wrong.
  • 3
    Get everything in writing before work starts. A signed contract should include the full scope of work, agreed price, payment schedule, start date, and estimated completion. No reputable tradesperson will refuse to provide one.
  • 4
    Be wary of quotes significantly below others. Get at least three quotes. A price dramatically lower than the rest rarely means a bargain. It often signals cut corners or a trader who has no intention of finishing the job.

Sarah Mitchell said the experience had been distressing not just financially but personally. "That garden was something we had built over thirty years. To see it dug up and left like that was genuinely upsetting."

The couple are urging other homeowners to make sure any additional costs during a job are agreed in writing before work continues. "The moment someone asks for more money than you agreed, stop. Get it in writing. Do not let work carry on until you have a signed amendment to the original contract."
