Consumer Warning - Groundworks

"He dug up our entire driveway, took £8,500, and we never saw him again": Bromsgrove family left with dangerous groundworks

The Mitchells from Stoke Heath paid a groundworker in full after he convinced them the job required immediate full payment. Three weeks later, half their property is an open trench and the trader is unreachable.
Consumer By Daniel Marsh, Consumer Affairs 5 min read

Sarah and Mark Mitchell wanted a new driveway and drainage system at their home in Stoke Heath. They found a groundworker through a recommendation in a local Facebook group, checked his reviews, and agreed a price of £8,500 for a full driveway replacement with new drainage channels.

"He came across as very experienced," said Mark, 54. "He talked us through exactly what needed doing, gave us a written quote on headed paper, and even suggested we pay in stages. We thought that was a good sign."

Work began promptly. The old driveway was broken up and excavated within two days. Then the trader said he needed full payment upfront for the materials, which he explained had to be ordered in bulk from a specific supplier.

"He had done everything right up to that point," said Sarah. "We felt we could trust him. So we paid the balance."

The materials never arrived. The trader stopped answering his phone on the fourth day. The Mitchells were left with an open excavation running the full width of their property, a pile of broken concrete, and no way of contacting the man they had paid.

An emergency call to a reputable groundworker to make the trench safe cost them a further £650 before any remedial work had even begun. Total estimated cost to complete the original job properly is now over £11,000.

"He had done everything right. He gave us a written quote, started on time, and suggested we pay in stages. That is why we trusted him with the final payment."

Mark Mitchell, Stoke Heath

Groundwork fraud is particularly damaging because the work involves significant disruption before anything of value is delivered. Once excavation has begun, homeowners can feel trapped into continuing payments for fear of being left with an unusable property.

Trading Standards say this is a deliberate tactic. "Rogue groundworkers will often begin work quickly to create a sense of commitment and dependency," said a spokesperson. "Once the homeowner feels they cannot walk away, requests for larger payments follow. At that point the leverage is entirely with the trader."

The Mitchells have filed reports with Worcestershire Trading Standards and Action Fraud. The trader had provided a home address on his quote, but when officers visited, the property was rented and the occupants had no knowledge of him.

What to do before hiring a groundworker
  • 1
    Do your due diligence. Check with your local Trading Standards for any complaints or warnings. Ask the tradesperson if they have a VIA number and verify it at viacheck.co.uk. And always ask for references from previous customers before committing to anything.
  • 2
    Never pay more than 10-20% upfront. Stagger payments to agreed milestones. Avoid cash wherever possible. Bank transfer creates a paper trail; cash leaves you with nothing if things go wrong.
  • 3
    Get everything in writing before work starts. A signed contract should include the full scope of work, agreed price, payment schedule, start date, and estimated completion. No reputable tradesperson will refuse to provide one.
  • 4
    Be wary of quotes significantly below others. Get at least three quotes. A price dramatically lower than the rest rarely means a bargain. It often signals cut corners or a trader who has no intention of finishing the job.

Mark Mitchell said he wanted other homeowners to know that even doing the basics correctly is not always enough. "We got a written quote. We agreed staged payments. He just bypassed that at the crucial moment and we were not firm enough."

"Never agree to pay in full for materials upfront, no matter how convincing the explanation," he said. "If a trader needs that kind of trust from you before the job is done, that is the moment to stop."
