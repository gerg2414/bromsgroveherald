Consumer Warning - Roofing

"He was at my door for ten minutes and gone with £4,500 by nightfall": Bromsgrove resident targeted by doorstep roofer

Sarah Mitchell, 58, from Stoke Heath paid her life savings to a roofer who turned up uninvited claiming to have spotted loose tiles. He promised to return the next morning to carry out the work. He never did.
Consumer By Daniel Marsh, Consumer Affairs 5 min read
Photo: The Bromsgrove Herald
Doorstep roofers often target elderly residents, claiming to have spotted damage whilst passing. Trading Standards say legitimate contractors never operate this way.

Sarah Mitchell was washing up when the knock came at her door in Stoke Heath last October. A man in a hi-vis vest introduced himself as a roofer who had been working on a property nearby and had noticed several loose tiles on her roof that could let in water before winter.

"He seemed very professional," said Sarah Mitchell, 58. "He had a clipboard, a business card, and he knew all the right things to say. He told me he could fit me in that afternoon while his crew were still in the area, and that it would save me booking someone out separately."

Sarah paid £4,500 in cash, the majority of her accessible savings. The man said he would collect materials and return within the hour. He did not come back.

When Sarah called the number on his card the following morning, it was disconnected. Her son later discovered the business name on the card had no Companies House registration, no website, and no traceable address.

"I felt such a fool," said Sarah. "But he had an answer for everything. He even mentioned the name of my neighbour to make it sound more believable. I never thought for a moment it was not genuine."

The matter has been reported to West Mercia Police and Worcestershire Trading Standards. Officers have linked the approach to a series of similar incidents targeting older residents across Bromsgrove and Redditch.

He mentioned my neighbour by name. He had a clipboard and a proper card. Everything about him said he was legitimate.

Sarah Mitchell, Stoke Heath

Doorstep roofing scams are among the most consistently reported forms of consumer fraud in Worcestershire, and they disproportionately target elderly and vulnerable residents. Trading Standards officers say the pattern is almost always identical: an uninvited approach, a plausible story, urgency, cash, and then disappearance.

"Legitimate roofing contractors do not cold call," said a Trading Standards spokesperson. "They do not knock on doors. They do not pressure you into a decision on the spot. If someone arrives uninvited claiming to have spotted a problem with your roof, the answer should always be to politely decline and find a contractor yourself in your own time."

Sarah's husband Mark, who lives nearby, said the hardest part was that his mother did not tell him for nearly three weeks. "She was embarrassed. She thought people would think she was foolish. That is exactly what these people count on."

What to do before hiring a roofer
  • 1
    Do your due diligence. Check with your local Trading Standards for any complaints or warnings. Ask the tradesperson if they have a VIA number and verify it at viacheck.co.uk. And always ask for references from previous customers before committing to anything.
  • 2
    Never pay more than 10-20% upfront. Stagger payments to agreed milestones. Avoid cash wherever possible. Bank transfer creates a paper trail; cash leaves you with nothing if things go wrong.
  • 3
    Get everything in writing before work starts. A signed contract should include the full scope of work, agreed price, payment schedule, start date, and estimated completion. No reputable tradesperson will refuse to provide one.
  • 4
    Be wary of quotes significantly below others. Get at least three quotes. A price dramatically lower than the rest rarely means a bargain. It often signals cut corners or a trader who has no intention of finishing the job.

Sarah is now speaking out to warn other residents in the Bromsgrove area, particularly those living alone, to be on their guard against uninvited tradespeople.

"If someone knocks on your door out of the blue, no matter how convincing they seem, do not hand over any money," said Sarah. "Call a family member, look the company up, and take your time. Anyone who pressures you to decide on the spot is not to be trusted."
